Mastering prototypes: an exercise

The techniques involved in maniuplating diagrams via their prototype structure, being unfamiliar, may require a bit of practice. So, we provide an example/exercise which illustrates these techniques.

The steps by which the former can be transformed into the latter are detailed in what follows. Please follow along, applying the operation associated with each step to the current item. If anything goes wrong, refresh the page, and start over (undo is coming soon, but is missing in this early release).

Step 1 (replacing prototypes): click "Swap Prototype" in the top bar. In the catalog that appears in the right-hand panel, select connector tab. Click and drag the arc arrow over any one of the existing arrows, until the existing arrow is highlighted. At that point do the drop (by releasing the mouse key). The result:

Step 2 (separation): Select the circle labeled "A". First verify that all the circles share the same protototype by clicking "Show Cohort" on the menu in the left panel. Then click "separate". This causes circle A to exit its cohort. It has its own prototype now - this can be verified by clicks on "Show Cohort" with circle A selected, or with any other circle selected. Next, color the prototype of A blue. For this purpose, select circle A and employ in the property editor in the right hand panel (adjust the bottom "Prototype" box, not the "Selected Item" box, of course). The result:

Step 3 (Local Swap): Yet again select the circle labeled "A", if it is not selected already. Click "Local Swap" the menu in the left-hand panel. This enters a mode in which each click on a compatible item moves that item into circle A's cohort, where it inherits from circle A's prototype (in this case, the visible sign is that the item turns blue) Click each of the other three inner circles. Finally, exit local swap mode. The result:

Step 4 (editing a property of a prototype). Now select any one of the outer circles. Set the fill of their prototype to red, using the same technique as was used when you painted the prototype of circle A blue. You're done! The result is the goal, once again:
